# Repsy — Target User and Brand Voice

## Primary persona — "Determined Rohan"

- Age 24 to 35
- Lives in an Indian metro or tier-2 city — Bangalore, Pune, Hyderabad, Mumbai, Delhi NCR, Jaipur, Indore, Lucknow, Chandigarh, Kochi
- Works a desk job in tech, finance, consulting, marketing, or product
- Earns between ₹8 lakh and ₹25 lakh per year
- Has been to the gym before but consistency has always been the problem
- Has tried two or three fitness apps and quit each within thirty days
- Has paid an influencer ₹2,000 to ₹5,000 for a "personalized plan" that turned out to be a generic PDF sold to thousands of others
- Owns a mid-range Android phone, most likely a Xiaomi, OnePlus, or Samsung
- Spends thirty to sixty minutes a day on Instagram and YouTube
- Knows what "creatine" and "progressive overload" mean
- Wants to look better in fitted shirts and stop feeling winded climbing four flights of stairs

## Secondary persona — "Returning Priya"

- Age 26 to 40, female, urban Indian
- Was active in college but lost the habit during career growth
- Has tried home workouts, Cult.fit, gym memberships — bounced off all of them
- Wants strength, not just weight loss
- Cares about form and safety; afraid of getting injured
- Deeply skeptical of fitness influencers and "before-and-after" marketing
- Will not engage with content that feels male-coded or aggressive

## What both personas have in common

- **They know what they don't know.** They want guidance, not motivation. Motivation isn't the bottleneck — direction is.
- **They have been burned before.** They've paid for generic plans. Trust is the hardest thing to earn back.
- **They have limited time.** A ninety-minute workout is a dealbreaker. Forty-five minutes is the cap.
- **They want measurable results.** Progress without visible measurement feels like nothing happened.
- **They are not impressed by hype.** They've seen every fitness app pitch. Generic claims bounce off them.

## Brand voice

### What to do

- Talk like a knowledgeable friend, not a coach barking instructions
- Be specific — use real numbers, real examples, real exercises
- Acknowledge that they have tried things before, and that those things didn't work for legitimate reasons
- Use second person constantly — "you," "your"
- Be honest about effort. Fitness is hard. This app doesn't make it easier; it makes it smarter
- Use light Indian-English phrasing where it lands naturally — "actually," "only," "no?"
- When making a claim, immediately back it with a feature that proves it

### What not to do

- Don't use hype words — "revolutionary," "game-changing," "unlock," "transform"
- Don't use bro-culture or military language — "crush," "dominate," "beast mode," "warrior," "destroy"
- Don't use fake-transformation language — "lose ten kilos in ten days"
- Don't be preachy about discipline, willpower, or "showing up"
- Don't moralize about food, weight, or appearance
- Don't use emojis in voiceover scripts — they don't translate to audio
- Don't over-promise — "made for you" is true because it actually is, not as a marketing claim

---

## Tone in one sentence

Like the smartest, most patient friend you have, who happens to also be a certified personal trainer and has read every fitness research paper of the last decade.
